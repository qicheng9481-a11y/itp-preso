# International Trade Policy Presentation

## Presentation Description
Slide-by-slide outline (12 min total + Q&A)

Slide 1 — Title & Roadmap (30 sec, all)
Title: "Three Endowments, Three Trade Patterns: H-O in Mongolia, Philippines, and Malaysia." Quick map showing the three countries; one-line teaser for each.

Slide 2 — Theory Recap: Heckscher-Ohlin & Specific Factors (1.5 min, Member 1)
H-O: countries export goods intensive in their abundant factor (capital/land/labor)
Specific-factors model: short-run version — factors tied to a sector benefit/lose from trade shifts even if H-O holds long-run
Set up the three lenses: land/resource-abundance (Mongolia), labor-abundance (Philippines), capital/skill-abundance (Malaysia)

Slide 3 — Mongolia: Resource Abundance & Specific-Factors Risk (2.5 min, Mongolian member)
Data: minerals ~87-90% of exports; coal alone ~55% of export value, 83.7 Mt in 2024, 90% to China
H-O lens: landlocked, resource-rich country exports land/mineral-intensive goods — textbook prediction confirmed
Specific-factors angle: mining capital and labor are "specific" to the sector; commodity price swings and China-demand dependence create boom-bust risk and exposure to a single trading partner
Policy hook: Mongolia's push to diversify export markets vs. its overwhelming gravity-driven (proximity) reliance on China

Slide 4 — Philippines: Labor Abundance, But Not Where You'd Expect (2.5 min, Filipino member)
Data: $38.34B remittances in 2024 (8.7% of GDP, highest in major ASEAN) vs. manufactured goods at ~79.5% of merchandise exports
H-O lens: the "export" of labor services (via migration) is itself a labor-abundance outcome — labor moves to where it's scarce rather than embodied in goods
Tension/independent angle: H-O predicts labor-intensive goods exports, but Philippines' comparative advantage manifests partly through labor mobility — a twist on the standard model (factor mobility vs. factor-content trade)
Specific-factors note: reliance on remittances vs. domestic labor-intensive manufacturing — distributional implications

Slide 5 — Malaysia: From Resources to Capital/Skill-Intensive GVCs (2.5 min, Malaysian member)
Data: E&E products 39.9% of exports (~$121B, 2024), historically transitioned from palm oil/rubber
H-O lens: shift toward capital- and skill-intensive electronics reflects capital accumulation and skill upgrading over decades — a moving H-O equilibrium
Bridge to imperfect competition: Malaysia's role isn't "produce a whole product" but specialize in stages (assembly/testing) of semiconductor GVCs — intra-industry trade, economies of scale, firm heterogeneity (Melitz) better explain why Malaysia trades ICs both ways rather than pure H-O

Slide 6 — Synthesis: One Framework, Three Outcomes (2 min, all together)
Side-by-side comparison: factor abundance → predicted export pattern → actual export pattern → policy implication, for all three countries
Key takeaway: H-O explains broad direction of trade (Mongolia, Philippines clear fits) but needs supplementing — specific-factors for distributional/volatility risk, new trade theory for Malaysia's GVC role
Closing: "endowments aren't static" — Malaysia shows how policy + capital accumulation can shift comparative advantage over time
